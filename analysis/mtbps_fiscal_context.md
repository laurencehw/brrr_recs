# 2025 MTBPS FISCAL CONTEXT
## Budgetary Framework for Economic Reform Recommendations

**Source:** National Treasury Medium-Term Budget Policy Statement, October 2025

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The 2025 MTBPS represents a **historic turning point** in South Africa's fiscal trajectory:

- **First debt stabilization since 2008**: Debt-to-GDP peaks at 77.9% in 2025/26 before declining
- **Improved fiscal position**: Budget deficit narrows from 4.7% to 2.7% of GDP by 2028/29
- **Tax revenue outperformance**: SARS collected R19.3bn above estimates
- **No new tax increases**: Fiscal consolidation achieved through improved collection and expenditure management

**Key Implication for Reform Agenda:** Limited new fiscal space requires prioritization of high-ROI, efficiency-enhancing reforms that can be funded within existing allocations or generate revenue/savings.

---

## KEY FISCAL METRICS

### Debt Outlook
- **Debt-to-GDP Ratio (2025/26):** 77.9% (stabilizing)
- **Trend:** First stabilization in 15 years, reversing post-2008 upward trajectory
- **Trajectory:** Projected to decline gradually from 2026/27 onwards

### Budget Balance
- **Budget Deficit (2025/26):** 4.7% of GDP (R345bn)
- **Budget Deficit (2028/29):** 2.7% of GDP (narrowing trend)
- **Primary Budget Surplus (2025):** R68.5bn (0.9% of GDP)
- **Primary Surplus (2028/29):** R224bn (growing)

### Economic Growth
- **GDP Growth (2025):** 1.2% (revised down from 1.4%)
- **GDP Growth (2026-2028):** 1.8% annually
- **Assessment:** Below potential, structural reforms needed to accelerate growth

### Revenue
- **Tax Revenue Performance:** R19.3bn above initial estimates (FY2024/25)
- **Tax Policy:** No new tax increases planned
- **SARS Performance:** Improved collections demonstrate administrative efficiency gains

### Inflation
- **New Inflation Target:** 3% (Â±1% band)
- **Significance:** First adjustment in 25 years, signals commitment to macroeconomic stability

## SECTOR-SPECIFIC BUDGET ALLOCATIONS

### Energy Sector
**Key Developments:**
- **Private Investment in Generation:** R30.78bn for 181 new facilities (1,401 MW capacity)
- **Koeberg Extension:** 20-year licence extension for Unit 2 (operation until 2045)
- **Transmission Infrastructure:** Independent Transmission Project Office (ITPO) established
- **Implication:** Energy sector reforms can leverage significant private capital; government role is regulatory enablement and transmission grid modernization

### Infrastructure
**Commitment:** R1 trillion multi-year infrastructure programme
- **Infrastructure Bond:** Target to raise R15bn for Budget Facility for Infrastructure
- **Priority Areas:** Energy, water, transport
- **Implication:** Major infrastructure investments planned, but efficient project management and anti-corruption measures critical

### Employment & Labour
**Recent Performance:**
- **Job Creation (Q3 2025):** 248,000 new jobs
- **Employment Level:** 17.1 million persons employed
- **Unemployment Rate:** 31.9% (down 1.3 percentage points)
- **Sectors:** Growth in construction, community/social services, trade
- **Implication:** Employment policies showing results; focus should be on sustaining and accelerating job creation

## FISCAL CONSTRAINTS & PRESSURES

### 1. **Limited Fiscal Space**
- Despite improved outlook, government faces spending ceilings
- New initiatives must be funded through reprioritization or efficiency gains
- No appetite for tax increases given economic growth constraints

### 2. **Debt Service Costs**
- Debt service costs remain elevated despite stabilization
- Interest payments consume significant portion of revenue
- Constrains ability to increase spending in other areas

### 3. **Wage Bill Pressures**
- Government wage bill remains major expenditure item
- Balancing fiscal consolidation with filling critical vacancies
- Public sector productivity improvements essential

### 4. **State-Owned Enterprise Contingent Liabilities**
- SOE financial health remains fiscal risk
- Government support must be balanced with fiscal sustainability
- Reform and operational efficiency improvements non-negotiable

### 5. **Social Spending Commitments**
- Social grants and basic services are protected
- Must maintain social safety net while pursuing fiscal consolidation
- Efficiency in service delivery critical to maintain spending levels

5. **Public Sector Efficiency**

## ALIGNMENT WITH BRRR RECOMMENDATIONS

### High Fiscal Alignment Areas

**Recommendations from BRRR reports that align with MTBPS priorities and fiscal realities:**

#### 1. **Administrative & Process Reforms (Low Cost, High Impact)**
- Budget execution improvements
- Compliance and reporting enhancements
- Procurement streamlining
- **Fiscal Feasibility:** HIGH - Can be funded within existing allocations

#### 2. **Revenue Enhancement (Self-Funding)**
- Irregular expenditure reduction
- Tax collection improvements
- Asset monetization
- **Fiscal Feasibility:** HIGH - Generate savings or revenue

#### 3. **Private Sector Leveraging (Low Fiscal Cost)**
- Private energy generation enablement
- Infrastructure public-private partnerships
- SME ecosystem development
- **Fiscal Feasibility:** HIGH - Mainly regulatory/enabling role

#### 4. **Capacity Building (Moderate Cost, High ROI)**
- Filling critical skill vacancies
- Training and development
- Systems modernization
- **Fiscal Feasibility:** MEDIUM - Requires targeted investment but high ROI

### Lower Fiscal Feasibility Areas

**Recommendations facing fiscal constraints:**

#### 1. **Large-Scale New Programmes**
- Major new spending initiatives
- Significant organizational expansions
- New infrastructure from government funds
- **Fiscal Feasibility:** LOW - Would require reprioritization or future fiscal space

#### 2. **Universal Service Expansions**
- Broad-based subsidy programmes
- Large-scale rollouts without efficiency gains
- **Fiscal Feasibility:** LOW-MEDIUM - Need to demonstrate sustainability

## FISCAL SCORING FRAMEWORK FOR RECOMMENDATIONS

### Fiscal Feasibility Categories

**Category 1: Fiscally Neutral or Positive (Highest Priority)**
- Administrative improvements
- Efficiency gains
- Revenue enhancements
- Anti-corruption measures
- *Can be implemented immediately within budget constraints*

**Category 2: Low Incremental Cost (<R100m per annum)**
- Targeted capacity building
- System improvements
- Pilot programmes
- *Fundable through reprioritization*

**Category 3: Moderate Cost (R100m-R1bn per annum)**
- Scaled interventions
- Infrastructure maintenance
- Multi-year programmes
- *Require careful business case and phasing*

**Category 4: High Cost (>R1bn per annum)**
- Major new initiatives
- Large infrastructure projects
- Comprehensive reforms
- *Require multi-year planning, external funding, or awaiting fiscal space*

## IMPLICATIONS FOR REFORM AGENDA

### Priority Recommendations (Fiscally Feasible)

Given fiscal constraints, the reform agenda should emphasize:

1. **"Quick Wins" - Fiscally Neutral Reforms**
   - 441 identified recommendations with low cost and high feasibility
   - Focus on administrative efficiency, compliance, process improvements
   - Can demonstrate reform momentum without fiscal strain

2. **Revenue/Savings Generating Reforms**
   - Irregular expenditure reduction (R221 recommendations across sectors)
   - Budget execution improvements (R616 recommendations)
   - Procurement efficiency (R248 recommendations)
   - Can self-fund or create fiscal space for other priorities

3. **Private Capital Leveraging**
   - Energy sector private investment enablement
   - Infrastructure PPPs
   - SME ecosystem support (regulatory, not financial)
   - Aligns with R1 trillion infrastructure commitment

4. **High-ROI Capacity Building**
   - Filling critical vacancies (246 recommendations)
   - Skills development in bottleneck areas
   - Targeted rather than broad-based expansion

### Sequencing Approach

**Phase 1 (0-6 months): Zero/Low Fiscal Cost**
- Administrative reforms
- Process improvements
- Compliance enhancements
- Anti-corruption measures

**Phase 2 (6-18 months): Revenue-Generating & Moderate Cost**
- Efficiency-driven reforms
- Targeted capacity building
- Revenue administration improvements
- Pilot programmes for larger reforms

**Phase 3 (18-36 months): Structural Reforms**
- Infrastructure scaling (with private capital)
- Major institutional reforms
- Large-scale programmes (as fiscal space emerges)

## CONCLUSION

The 2025 MTBPS provides both **constraints and opportunities**:

**Constraints:**
- Limited fiscal space for new major spending
- Need to maintain fiscal consolidation path
- Debt stabilization must be preserved

**Opportunities:**
- Improved revenue collection creates modest room for reprioritization
- Strong focus on infrastructure investment
- Political will for efficiency and reform
- Private sector capital available (especially energy)

**Strategic Implication:**
The reform agenda must be **ruthlessly prioritized** toward:
1. High-impact, low-cost administrative reforms
2. Revenue/savings generating measures
3. Private capital leveraging
4. Targeted capacity building in strategic areas

This fiscal discipline actually **strengthens the reform agenda** by forcing focus on highest-ROI interventions and demonstrable results.
